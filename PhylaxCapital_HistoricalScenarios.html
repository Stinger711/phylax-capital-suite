Q4 2008 — Global Financial Crisis
1 October 2008 → 19 December 2008  ·  79 days (Q4 expiry)
The collapse of Lehman Brothers on September 15 triggered the worst financial crisis since the Great Depression. By October 2008, credit markets had frozen globally. The ALSI had already fallen from its May 2008 peak of ~32,500 pts, but the October–November period saw the most violent single-quarter destruction in modern JSE history. JIBAR peaked at 12.5% as the SARB held rates before beginning a cutting cycle in December. Volatility was elevated but pre-spike vol of ~24% was used for the entry pricing — the SAVI equivalent would have spiked to 40–50% after entry, massively increasing the value of the put positions.

Q1 2020 — COVID-19 Pandemic
2 January 2020 → 19 March 2020  ·  77 days
The fastest bear market in modern history. From an all-time high of ~59,000 pts in early January, the ALSI collapsed 35.6% in under 11 weeks as the WHO declared a global pandemic on March 11. South Africa announced a national lockdown on March 23. The violence of the sell-off was unprecedented — the JSE fell over 10% in a single session on March 18. JIBAR stood at 6.75% (SARB then cut 300bps between March and July 2020). Entry implied vol of ~16.5% was exceptionally low — markets had priced almost no tail risk. This is why the option budget bought 233 contracts: the options were cheap, making the eventual payout extraordinary relative to cost.

The COVID scenario illustrates the most important principle: Options bought when volatility is low (16.5%) and markets are calm are the cheapest and most effective protection. The R409k option budget — just 1.6% of the R25m sleeve — funded puts that paid out R35.2m. This is why systematic, unconditional quarterly rolling is essential: you cannot know in advance when the next shock will arrive, and waiting for vol to spike before buying protection means you buy at exactly the wrong time.

Q2 2022 — Ukraine Invasion & Rate Shock
1 April 2022 → 30 June 2022  ·  91 days (Q2 expiry)
Russia's invasion of Ukraine on February 24 2022 shocked commodity and credit markets globally. While the JSE benefited briefly from commodity exposure, aggressive Fed rate hikes (fastest hiking cycle since 1980) and domestic load-shedding concerns dragged the ALSI lower through Q2. From its January 2022 high of ~74,500, the ALSI fell 8.9% to close the quarter at ~67,900 — just above the 10% OTM strike of 67,050. This scenario illustrates the capital guarantee: options expired worthless, but the fund still returned capital in full, and the DFM portfolio was marginally better off than an unhedged equivalent due to the carve-out avoiding some equity market exposure.

This is the "cost" scenario — and it is the most important one to show DFMs. The ALSI fell 8.9%, ending 850 pts above the strike. Options expired worthless. The fund returned −1.1% (just the foregone JIBAR yield on the option budget = R274k). But the portfolio was still R1.94m better off than an unhedged portfolio, because the 5% carve-out capital was not exposed to equity market decline. The maximum cost of Macro Protector in a year where nothing triggers is the JIBAR yield on the option budget — not the full allocation. This is the asymmetric cost structure that makes the strategy compelling.
