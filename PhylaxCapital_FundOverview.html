01 — The Problem

Sharp Drawdowns Are Not Rare — They Are Scheduled

Analysis of 20 years of JSE All Share monthly return data (2005–2024, 240 months) reveals a pattern that most investors underestimate: large, sudden drawdowns occur with far greater frequency than traditional risk models suggest.

20-Year ALSI Crash Frequency

15 rolling 3-month windows exceeded a 10% decline — one every 16 months on average. Six distinct crash events in 20 years gives an average gap of 3.3 years between major events. A DFM on a 5-year mandate will statistically encounter at least two qualifying crash periods.

The critical insight is not just frequency — it is timing. The COVID-19 crash arrived when the SAVI implied volatility index was at a multi-year low of 16.5%. Markets had priced almost zero tail risk. By the time volatility spiked above 50%, buying the same protection had become four times more expensive and the crash had already occurred. Waiting for a signal to hedge is not a strategy — it is a guarantee of buying protection at exactly the wrong time.

02 — The Solution

Permanent, Unconditional Protection With No Timing Risk

Macro Protector is structured as a permanent 3–7% carve-out from a DFM's equity allocation. It operates identically to the Universa Investments approach deployed for US institutional investors — a small, systematically managed sleeve that converts crash events into capital windfalls while protecting the rest of the portfolio.

What It Is

  • Capital guaranteed: worst case is 0% nominal (before fees) at every quarterly exit
  • Asymmetric payoff: modest cost in quiet markets, multiples of cost in crashes
  • Systematic rolling: no timing decisions — protection always in place
  • Exchange traded: SAFEX-listed ALSI options, centrally cleared

What It Is Not

  • Not a market hedge: gradual bear markets may not trigger payouts
  • Not a volatility fund: it buys protection, not volatility exposure
  • Not leveraged: cannot return less than 0% (before fees) at quarterly exit
  • Not discretionary: no tactical positioning or market timing

The fund is modelled on Universa Investments' proposition, which demonstrated a 3.3% allocation reducing COVID portfolio drawdown from −35% to −10% for institutional clients. Macro Protector delivers the same structural concept within the South African RIHF regulatory framework using SAFEX-listed instruments.

03 — How It Works

The 90/10 Capital Guarantee Structure

Every quarter, the fund sizes a JIBAR-linked fixed rate note to mature at exactly NAV at the quarterly exit date. The interest earned in advance on that note — not the principal — funds the purchase of out-of-the-money put options. This means the option sleeve costs nothing beyond foregone yield.

Capital Guarantee Sizing
Cash Core = NAV ÷ (1 + JIBAR × T)
Option Budget = NAV − Cash Core = JIBAR interest earned in advance

Worst case: options expire worthless → Cash Core matures to NAV → return = 0% nominal
Option Budget Example (JIBAR 7.5%, NAV R25m, T = 90/365)
Cash Core = R25,000,000 ÷ 1.018495 = R24,546,066
Option Budget = R453,934 (the JIBAR interest, ~1.8% of NAV per quarter)
Strike = ALSI × 90% (10% out of the money at entry)

Rolling Before the Theta Cliff

Options are not held to expiry. Each position is sold before the theta cliff — the point where daily time-value decay accelerates beyond 0.5% of option value per day. For a 90-day put, this typically occurs at 40–50 days before expiry; for longer-dated puts, the cliff is proportionally later.

  • Buy: puts with 90 days to the quarterly SAFEX expiry, 10% OTM
  • Roll: sell at market price (intrinsic + time value + vol spike premium) before cliff
  • Replace: buy fresh puts for the next quarterly expiry, resetting the clock

Selling before expiry captures more value in a crash because implied volatility spikes during market dislocations — the BS market price at T−45 days includes both intrinsic value and the elevated time value caused by the vol spike. Historical scenario payouts in this document use intrinsic-at-expiry as a conservative floor.

04 — The Mathematics

Black-Scholes with Dividend Yield Correction

All option pricing uses the Merton (1973) modification of Black-Scholes incorporating the ALSI continuous dividend yield (q = 3.8% p.a.). Omitting the dividend yield overstates the number of contracts purchasable by approximately 25% — a critical error that would misrepresent both cost and payout.

Black-Scholes Put Price (Merton 1973)
d₁ = [ ln(S/K) + (r − q + σ²/2) × T ] ÷ (σ × √T)
d₂ = d₁ − σ × √T

Put (pts) = K × e^(−rT) × N(−d₂) − S × e^(−qT) × N(−d₁)
Put (R/contract) = Put(pts) × R10

Variables: S = ALSI spot · K = strike (10% OTM) · r = JIBAR · σ = IV (SAVI)
T = 90/365 years · q = 3.8% ALSI div yield · N(x) = normal CDF
Verified Test Case (Python/scipy)
S=113 000 · K=101 700 · r=7.5% · σ=18% · T=0.2466yr · q=3.8%
→ d₁=1.3256 · d₂=1.2362 · Put=447.7 pts = R4 477/contract
→ Delta=−0.0924 · Contracts at R25m budget = 413

The COVID Lesson — Buy When It Is Cheap

In Q1 2020 the option budget was just R409,284 — 1.6% of the R25m sleeve — because implied volatility was at a multi-year low of 16.5%. That budget funded puts that paid out R35.2m. Had the same position been entered in March 2020 after volatility spiked above 50%, the identical budget would have bought fewer than 30 contracts. Systematic unconditional quarterly rolling is the only mechanism that ensures you are positioned before the crash arrives.

Key Risks

Options Expire OTM

If ALSI does not breach the strike at roll date, payout is zero. Fund returns capital at 0% nominal (before fees). This occurred in 2022.

High Entry Volatility

Elevated SAVI at entry reduces contracts purchasable for the same budget, reducing potential payout in a subsequent crash.

Intra-Quarter Crash Recovery

A crash that occurs and recovers entirely before the roll date may not trigger a meaningful payout.

SAFEX Execution

Bid/offer spreads on deep OTM ALSI puts may result in execution above BS theoretical mid-market. Bid haircut tiers apply.
